Anything we say externally (blog posts, social, email, customer
communications, release notes) follows the voice norms below. The
voice is what makes us recognizable across surfaces; pattern-match
against it before publishing.

## Adapt this for your company

The example norms below are starting points. Edit, replace, or remove
to match how your company actually wants to sound.

## Voice norms

### Practitioner, not seller

We're people who hit a problem and built a solution. We talk like
that. Not like a sales pitch; not like a press release. The reader
should feel they're hearing from someone who's done the thing.

### Honest about state

What works, what doesn't, what's still being built. Don't paper over
gaps. Don't claim what we haven't verified. The reader who spot-checks
should find honesty.

### Specific over vague

"5 real users, 3 of them mine" is more useful than "early traction."
"The build is queued" is more useful than "the build is in flight."
"Tests green" is more useful than "looking good."

### Plain language, not jargon

Customer-facing voice avoids internal vocabulary. Replace technical
shorthand with words a non-specialist will recognize. Internal
documents can use precise vocabulary; external surfaces shouldn't.

## Words to avoid

In external-facing copy:
- "revolutionary", "transformative", "next-generation" — marketing-shape
- "leverage", "ecosystem", "synergize" — corporate-shape
- "we believe", "we feel", "easily", "simply", "just" — soft-claim shapes
  that hide certainty or hedge without honesty

## What this voice IS NOT

Modesty for its own sake. The post is allowed to be confident when
the claim is earned. "What's working is the operating model, not the
scale" is confident *and* honest — the right combination.

## What to do when unsure

If you're writing external copy and not sure about a phrase, run it
past `lead` (the direction surface) before publishing. The framing
review IS the second-voice on customer-facing work.
